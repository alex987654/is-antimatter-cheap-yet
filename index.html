The lowest possible cost comes from the energy locked in the mass (Einstein's relationship E=mc^2), divided by how efficiently we can produce antimatter, then multiplied by the electricity price and a capital-overhead factor. The result is shown as a full range of outcomes built from 12000 randomized trials (a method called Monte Carlo simulation), alongside a sandbox of 22 cost-reducing inventions.

Looking ahead · projected future cost decline (speculative)
Treat this as a smoothed background trend, not a forecast. The decline compounds each decade (it does not fall in a straight line), and it is set per decade on purpose — year-to-year change is not something we can realistically resolve. In reality cost would not glide down smoothly at all; it would sit roughly flat and then drop abruptly as each new breakthrough lands. Those discrete jumps are what the invention catalog below models — this slider is only the gentle trend underneath them.

Sensitivity · which input drives the uncertainty
Each bar shows how far the result swings (in orders of magnitude — that is, factors of ten) when that one input is moved by a single standard deviation while the others are held fixed. The production efficiency usually dominates, so how reliable any estimate is depends on pinning that efficiency down.

How the number is built: cost per gram = (the energy locked in one gram of mass, 9×10¹³ joules, divided by the production efficiency) × the electricity price (in dollars per joule) × a capital-and-labor overhead factor × an optional future-decline factor × the combined effect of any inventions you apply. The efficiency, the overhead, and the electricity price are each varied randomly (using a log-normal spread — bell-shaped on a logarithmic scale) across thousands of trials; efficiency is capped at 100% (the energy floor). The tool reproduces the range of published estimates by design — it is not a measurement. Market data is editable and current as of about 18 June 2026. The shipment section is based on CERN's first antimatter transport — 92 antiprotons moved by truck on 24 March 2026. The inventions are illustrative adjustments to the inputs, not forecasts.
